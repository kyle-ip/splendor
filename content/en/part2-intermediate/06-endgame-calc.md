15 points is not the finish line—it starts the **endgame countdown**.

## Trigger First vs Chase Points

**Trigger first** (you reach 15+):

- Opponents still get their remaining turns this round
- When ahead by 5+, triggering early can **lock in** the lead
- When ahead by 2–3, estimate opponents’ max score in one turn

**Chase points** (opponent is about to trigger):

- Calculate your **max prestige in one turn** (buy + noble)
- If you still can’t catch up, you should have set up a high scorer or noble the turn before

## Pre-Endgame Checklist

1. Can I buy to the threshold next turn?
2. After I trigger, how many opponents haven’t acted yet?
3. Do opponents have uncashed high-point cards in reserve?
4. Should I **buy 3 points now** instead of taking gems toward a 5 (which gives opponents another round)?

## Worked Math

| Player | Prestige now | This turn option | Result |
|--------|--------------|------------------|--------|
| You | 13 | Buy a 3-point card | 16 → trigger endgame |
| Opponent | 12 | Could buy a 5 next turn | At most 17 if they still get a turn |

**Seat order matters.** If you trigger and the opponent has **already acted** this round, they do not get another turn—your 16 likely holds. If they act **after** you, check whether 12 + 5 (+ noble?) can beat 16.

**Competitive default when ahead:** take the sure 3 and trigger rather than gambling on a slower 5.

## Chapter Takeaways

- Engine → nobles → tempo → reserve → player count → endgame
- Use the noble tracker and card-value tool to pressure-test close races
- Remember the tiebreak: fewest development cards wins
