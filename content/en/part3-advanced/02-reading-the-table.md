Most of Splendor is open information. Bonuses, tokens (with colors), prestige, and the display are public; **reserved card faces stay hidden**. Reading what is visible is a core competitive skill.

## Readable Information

| Information | Inference |
|-------------|-----------|
| Opponent bonuses | Which colors they may buy next |
| Reserve count | Intent to buy next turn (faces unknown) |
| Tokens in hand | Colors and counts—purchase power and denial targets |
| Display gaps | Who urgently needs a given card |

## 15-Second Checklist

Every turn, scan in this order:

1. Who is **closest to a noble**?
2. Which display card is a **unique answer** critical for an opponent?
3. Who **leads in prestige** and might trigger next turn?
4. Does **my best buy** conflict with someone else's?
5. After the next refill, which color becomes more contested?

## Inference Drills

### Drill A — Next buy

Opponent: sapphire ×2, ruby ×1 bonuses; tokens show 2 sapphire, 1 gold, 2 diamond.  
Display: Level 1 +1 sapphire costing 3 sapphire before bonuses.

**Read:** Net cost is 1 sapphire—they can buy now (tokens + gold). If you need that card, contest it **this turn**.

### Drill B — Reserve threat

Opponent: 12 prestige, **2 cards** in reserve, tokens look rainbow-rich.  
You: 13 prestige, can buy a 2-pt now or take gems toward a 5.

**Read:** Hidden reserves may cash 3–5 + noble. Prefer the line that keeps you ahead after their next buy—often the sure points now.

### Drill C — Noble race

Two opponents share emerald/ruby on a 3/3/3; one has emerald ×2 ruby ×2, the other emerald ×3 ruby ×1.  
Display shows cheap +1 ruby.

**Read:** Second player is one ruby from the noble. Buying or taking the last useful ruby gems is high leverage—especially in 2p.

## Board Example

Opponent A: 2 sapphire and 2 ruby bonuses, 8 gems in hand (colors visible), 10 prestige.  
Display: a 4-point card needing 3 sapphire 2 ruby—easy for them.

**Inference**: A will likely buy it next turn. Consider buying it first, reserving it, or taking bank sapphire.

## Caution

Reading the table serves **your decisions**—not denial for its own sake. If snatching a card is useless to you and expensive, stick to your own path.
