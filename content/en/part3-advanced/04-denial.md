Denial is a competitive tool, but it should not **maliciously stall** the game.

## Legal Denial Methods

- Buy a card an opponent urgently needs (best if you can use it too)
- Take key gems from the bank
- Reserve the unique answer
- Reach a noble first

## ROI by Player Count

| Count | Denial ROI | Why |
|------:|------------|-----|
| 2 | **Highest** | Every slowdown hits your only rival |
| 3 | Medium | Blocking A may help B’s parallel path |
| 4 | Lowest per action | One denial rarely stops the table; your buy tempo matters more |

**Default:** In 4p, deny only when the play **also advances your engine or score**. In 2p, pure gem denial is often correct.

## When Denial Is Wrong

Denial fails when:

| Situation | Better play |
|-----------|-------------|
| The card is useless to you **and** expensive | Take gems toward *your* next buy |
| Opponent is not actually close (wrong color read) | Build your own path |
| Blocking A leaves B a free Level 3 | Prefer scoring yourself |
| You spend two turns with zero constructive buys | Stop—resume engine |

Quick check: *If this denial didn’t exist, would I still want this action?* If no, it’s usually a trap.

## Worked Spot

| Fact | Value |
|------|-------|
| Opponent bonuses | emerald ×2, sapphire ×2 |
| Noble on table | emerald ×3, sapphire ×3, onyx ×3 |
| Display | Level 1 +1 emerald, cheap for you |

If you can buy that emerald card **and** it helps your own path, do it. You slow their noble and strengthen yourself.

If the card is useless to you and expensive, prefer taking the last sapphire tokens they need—or reserve only when you have a real buy plan.

## Don’t Overdo It

- Buying expensive cards **only** to block an opponent when they’re useless to you
- Multiple turns with no constructive buys
- Creating table disputes over “bad sportsmanship”

## Efficient Denial

Prefer **two birds with one stone**: cards that help your engine or score while hurting an opponent.

## Mindset

When denied, stay calm and switch paths. The Splendor display changes every turn—there’s often a Plan B.
