Follow one compressed 2-player line. Not every gem take is listed—focus on **decision points**. Seat order: You → Opponent.

This line is a **hybrid open → 3/3/3 noble sprint** (see [Opening Paths](/learn/advanced/part3-advanced-05-openings)). Opponent plays mono blue depth. For seat-order with a third actor, see the [3-player sample](/learn/advanced/part3-advanced-06-sample-game-3p).

## Setup Snapshot

| | You | Opponent |
|--|-----|----------|
| Opening intent | Hybrid → 3/3/3 noble if colors open | Blue depth |

**Nobles:** green×3 red×3 black×3; green×4 red×4; white×4 blue×4.  
**Early Level 1:** cheap green, cheap red, two blue, expensive black mono.

## Snapshot After Turn 5

| | You | Opponent |
|--|-----|----------|
| Bonuses | green 1, red 1, black 1 (or reserve instead of black) | blue 2 |
| Hand (approx.) | 1–2 flex gems + gold if reserved | blue-heavy |
| Prestige | 0 | 0 |
| Path | One buy from finishing 3/3/3 colors | Tall blue + Level 2 points |

Use this table to rehearse: if your T5 black buy missed, you should still be **closer to width**; they should be **closer to depth**.

## Turns 1–5 — Opening

| Turn | Actor | Play | Why |
|------|-------|------|-----|
| 1 | You | Take green + red + white | Soft open toward 3/3/3 or 4/4; keep white for flex |
| 1 | Opp | Take 2 blue (bank ≥4) | Commits mono / white×4 blue×4 |
| 2 | You | Buy cheap green | First engine piece; 3/3/3 color |
| 2 | Opp | Buy cheap blue | Same idea on their color |
| 3 | You | Buy cheap red | Two 3/3/3 colors live |
| 3 | Opp | Take blue-heavy mix | Depth over noble width |
| 4 | You | Reserve a card and take 1 gold | Bridge toward 3/3/3; gold for gaps |
| 4 | Opp | Buy second blue | Engine ahead on one color |
| 5 | You | Buy cheap black (or reserve if none) | Third 3/3/3 color—or pivot soft |

**Callout:** After T5 you are closer to a **3/3/3**; opponent is closer to a **tall blue** and possibly white×4 blue×4 later. Neither should force green×4 red×4 yet—green/red depth is still thin.

## Turns 6–10 — Midgame Pivot

| Turn | Actor | Play | Why |
|------|-------|------|-----|
| 6 | You | Buy third 3/3/3 bonus / finish gaps | Noble sprint is real |
| 6 | Opp | Level 2 blue-ish 2-pt if affordable | Points + bonus while you chase noble |
| 7 | You | Attract green×3 red×3 black×3 (+3) after a buy | Efficient scoring; table nobles change |
| 7 | Opp | Take toward Level 2/3 or deny your next red | 2p denial ROI is high |
| 8 | You | Don’t cling to green×4 red×4 | 4/4 needs more depth; pivot to points |
| 8 | Opp | Contested reserve on your Level 2 answer | Legal denial if they can buy soon |
| 9 | You | Buy flexible Level 2 (2–3 pts) | Post-noble: score, don’t rebuild 0-pt forever |
| 9 | Opp | Cash own Level 2 | Race toward 15 |
| 10 | You | Scan prestige + seats | Ask: trigger next turn or setup 5? |

**Callout:** Winning the first noble is not winning the game. The player who **converts bonuses into Level 2 points** after the noble usually leads the race.

## Turns 11–15 — Endgame

| Turn | Actor | Play | Why |
|------|-------|------|-----|
| 11 | You | Buy 3 pts to ~13–14 if available | Prefer sure progress over slow 5 setup |
| 11 | Opp | Same pressure | 2p endgames are short |
| 12 | You | Check: if you hit 15, does Opp still act? | Seat matrix from Endgame Calculation |
| 12 | Opp | Noble or 5-pt if it overtakes | Hidden reserves matter—count them |
| 13–15 | Both | Cash / deny / tiebreak-aware buys | Avoid useless 0-pt buys into a card-count tie |

**Sample finish:** You trigger at 16 after Opp already acted → Opp gets no extra turn → your leaner card count also covers the tiebreak if scores meet.

## What to Rehearse

1. Opening scan: 4/4 vs 3/3/3 pressure  
2. Dual-purpose buys (engine + denial)  
3. Post-noble pivot to Level 2 economy  
4. Seat-order trigger math  

Replay the same skeleton with different Level 1 refills—notice how often hybrid beats a forced mono when nobles disagree with the display.

Rehearse the same 2p tempo—noble sprint, contested reserves, seat-order triggers—in [standard practice](/tools/standard) (2 players vs AI) when you want a full table without a partner.
